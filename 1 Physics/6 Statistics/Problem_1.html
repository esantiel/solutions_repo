Exploring the Central Limit Theorem Through Simulations

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) tells us that, under certain conditions, the distribution of sample means will approximate a normal distribution as the sample size grows, regardless of the population’s original shape. Let’s explore this with simulations!

Step 1: Simulating Sampling Distributions

We’ll simulate three population distributions:

  • Uniform Distribution: Flat, even spread (0 to 10).
  • Exponential Distribution: Skewed, right-tailed (scale = 2).
  • Binomial Distribution: Discrete (n=10, p=0.5).

Step 2: Sampling and Visualization

For each population, we drew samples of sizes 5, 10, 30, and 50, computed the means, and repeated this 1,000 times. Here are the results:

Uniform Distribution

Even at n=5, it’s bell-shaped. By n=30, it’s nearly normal.

Exponential Distribution

Skewed at n=5, but normal-like by n=50.

Binomial Distribution

Bumpy at n=5 due to discreteness, smooth by n=50.

Step 3: Parameter Exploration

  • Shape Influence: Skewed distributions (exponential) converge slower than symmetric ones.
  • Sample Size: Larger n (30+) yields normality.
  • Variance Impact: Spread shrinks with larger n (standard error = σ/√n).

Step 4: Practical Applications

The CLT powers real-world stats:

  1. Estimating Parameters: Confidence intervals for means.
  2. Quality Control: Monitoring averages in manufacturing.
  3. Financial Models: Normal-based predictions.

Discussion

These simulations confirm the CLT: sample means turn normal as n grows. Skewness slows convergence, but the outcome holds. The tighter spread with larger n matches theory perfectly. This makes the CLT a cornerstone of statistics!
